## SEARCH 1: VP of Customer Success — Series B Healthcare SaaS

### Role Context (recruiter enters in form)

**Role title:** VP of Customer Success
**Company:** Clarion Health (fictional)
**Hiring manager / stakeholders:** Sarah Chen (CEO/co-founder), Marcus Williams (COO), Dr. Priya Mehta (Chief Medical Officer)

**What does this role need to accomplish in the first 12 months?**
Build the CS function from scratch. Clarion has 42 enterprise health system clients paying $150K-$800K ARR. Currently the CEO handles the top 10 accounts personally and 3 "support specialists" (really account coordinators with no CS training) handle the rest. NRR is 94% — not terrible but not where it needs to be for their Series C story. They need someone who can hire a team of 5-6, build a renewal playbook, reduce churn from 6% to under 3%, and take the CEO out of day-to-day client management without any of the top 10 accounts feeling abandoned.

**Top priorities (ranked):**
1. Take over CEO's top 10 accounts without any relationship damage
2. Build and hire CS team (5-6 people) in first 6 months
3. Implement a renewal and expansion playbook — they have zero process
4. Get NRR above 105% by month 12
5. Establish QBR cadence with all enterprise accounts

**Compensation range:** $280K-$340K base + 0.3-0.5% equity
**Location:** Remote, NYC or Boston preferred for quarterly on-sites
**Company stage:** Series B ($45M raised), 85 employees, founded 2021

**What happened with the last person in this seat?**
There was no last person. The CEO has been doing this herself. She's self-aware that she's the bottleneck and that her involvement doesn't scale, but she's also deeply protective of the client relationships she built. The COO told us privately that Sarah has tried to delegate client management twice before to internal people and pulled them back within weeks because "they didn't get the clinical context." This is the real landmine — the hire needs to earn Sarah's trust quickly or she'll never actually let go.

**What would make this hire fail?**
Someone who treats healthcare clients like generic enterprise accounts. These are hospital CIOs and CMOs — they have zero patience for vendor BS and they expect the person managing their account to understand clinical workflows at a conversational level. Also: someone who needs heavy structure handed to them. There's no playbook, no CRM hygiene, no defined handoff from sales. The person has to be comfortable building in ambiguity.

**Recruiter-only notes (never injected):**
Sarah can be a micromanager when she's anxious about client relationships. Marcus (COO) is the more reasonable voice and will be this person's actual day-to-day ally. The CMO sits in on final interviews but doesn't have veto power — she's evaluating clinical fluency. Comp is firm at the top — they won't go above $340K base even for a perfect candidate. Two other firms are also running this search (Caldwell and a boutique in Boston).

## SEARCH 2: Chief Revenue Officer — Growth-Stage Enterprise Software

### Role Context (recruiter enters in form)

**Role title:** Chief Revenue Officer
**Company:** Nuvolo (fictional — loosely inspired by real enterprise software companies)
**Hiring manager / stakeholders:** Michael Torres (CEO/founder), Board (lead: Alison Park at Sequoia)

**What does this role need to accomplish in the first 12 months?**
Nuvolo is at $38M ARR growing 45% YoY. They sell connected workplace solutions to Fortune 500 facilities teams. They have a strong product-market fit and a loyal customer base but the go-to-market is a mess. Two co-heads of sales (field + inside) who don't talk to each other. Marketing reports to the CEO. CS reports to the COO. Nobody owns the full revenue number. The board is pushing for a CRO to unify sales, marketing, CS, and partnerships under one leader before the Series C raise (targeted for Q1 2027). The CEO is a product visionary who hates managing the commercial org.

**Top priorities (ranked):**
1. Unify sales, marketing, and CS into a single revenue org within 90 days
2. Fix the field/inside sales conflict — align comp plans, territories, handoffs
3. Get the company to $55M ARR by end of FY (from $38M)
4. Build the pitch deck and revenue story for the Series C
5. Hire a VP of Marketing (currently a team of 4 with no leader)

**Compensation range:** $350K-$425K base + significant equity (0.75-1.2%)
**Location:** Austin, TX (HQ) — 3 days/week in office required
**Company stage:** Series B ($62M raised), 180 employees, founded 2018

**What happened with the last person in this seat?**
No prior CRO. The CEO has been acting as de facto revenue leader. He's exhausted and the board told him in the last meeting that his #1 job is to hire a CRO and get out of the way. The two sales co-heads were both considered for the role and passed over — one is a flight risk, the other will be fine reporting to a CRO as long as they're respected.

**What would make this hire fail?**
A pure sales leader who doesn't understand CS as a revenue function. Someone who comes in and blows up what's working — the sales team is actually quite good at winning deals, they just can't coordinate. A big-company CRO who needs a massive support structure. And honestly, someone who can't manage Michael — he'll stay in deals and override decisions unless the CRO earns his trust and pushes back firmly.

**Recruiter-only notes (never injected):**
The board has a strong opinion that the CRO needs to come from enterprise software — not consumer, not SMB, not services. Alison at Sequoia specifically said "I want someone who's run a $50M+ revenue org at a company that sells to F500." The inside sales co-head (Derek Voss) is the flight risk — he's been talking to recruiters. If he leaves before the CRO starts, that's a problem. Michael is a fantastic product CEO but a terrible manager of commercial people. He gives contradictory feedback, changes direction mid-quarter, and occasionally calls customers directly to pitch features that don't exist yet. The CRO needs to manage this without making Michael feel managed.

## SEARCH 3: Board Director — Public Company (Beth Stewart / Trewstar-style)

### Role Context (recruiter enters in form)

**Role title:** Independent Board Director
**Company:** MedAlliance Corp (fictional — public, mid-cap medical devices)
**Hiring manager / stakeholders:** Board Chair (Robert Hadley), Governance Committee, CEO (Jennifer Walsh)

**What does this role need to accomplish in the first 12 months?**
The board needs a director with deep digital transformation experience. MedAlliance is a $2.1B medical device company that's been hardware-focused for 40 years and is now trying to build a SaaS platform for connected surgical instruments. They've acquired two small software companies and the integration is struggling. The board has zero software expertise. They need someone who can evaluate the CTO's software strategy, provide governance on the SaaS transition, and ask the hard questions the rest of the board can't.

**Top priorities (ranked):**
1. Evaluate and advise on the SaaS platform strategy
2. Provide oversight on the two software acquisitions integration
3. Serve on the Technology Committee (currently has no tech-savvy members)
4. Bring digital transformation perspective to strategic planning
5. Mentor the CEO on software business models (she's a medical device veteran, not a software person)

**Compensation range:** $280K-$320K annual retainer + equity grant
**Location:** Board meets quarterly in Minneapolis + 2 annual off-sites. Otherwise remote.
**Company:** NYSE-listed, $2.1B revenue, 8,500 employees

**What happened with the last person in this seat?**
The director who retired was a 72-year-old former hospital CEO. Great for clinical credibility, zero help on technology. The governance committee explicitly said "we do not want another healthcare operator — we want a technology leader who can learn healthcare, not a healthcare leader who claims to understand technology."

**What would make this appointment fail?**
Someone who tries to run the software strategy instead of governing it. A board director who confuses advisory with operational authority. Also: someone without public company board experience — the governance requirements are specific and learning on the job at this level is risky.

**Recruiter-only notes (never injected):**
The board is under pressure from an activist investor (Starboard Value owns 4.2%) who's been publicly critical of the software acquisition strategy. This board appointment is partly defensive — they need someone credible enough that Starboard can't claim the board lacks technology competence. Jennifer (CEO) is privately skeptical of the SaaS pivot but is being pushed by the board and Starboard. The new director needs to navigate that tension. Also: the governance committee has a strong preference for a female candidate to improve board diversity (currently 7M/2F).

## USAGE NOTES FOR TESTING

### How to use these scenarios

**Role input form testing:**
- Enter Search 1, 2, or 3 role context into the form
- Upload the simulated JD and stakeholder notes as text files
- Verify the form handles all field types, document categories, and edge cases

**Session generation testing:**
- Feed the role context to the session generation engine
- Verify it produces tailored questions (not generic ones)
- Check that Search 1 generates healthcare-fluency questions, Search 2 generates org-unification questions, Search 3 generates governance questions

**Candidate discovery testing:**
- Run Candidates A-F through the discovery session
- Verify the injected primer shapes the conversation without being obvious
- Check that the output lens document captures role-relevant signal

**Recruiter review testing:**
- Verify the recruiter can see the full lens output
- Test the annotation interface — can the recruiter add notes per section?
- Verify recruiter-only notes stay separate from candidate-visible content

**Brief generation testing:**
- Generate candidate briefs for A, B, and C against Search 1
- Check that the brief highlights match/tension against the role context
- Verify the brief is polished enough to forward to a client

### Edge cases to test
- Candidate C (Dr. Kim) has no CS title — does the system handle non-traditional backgrounds?
- Candidate E (Ryan Park) is from a much larger company — does the system flag scale mismatch?
- Search 3 is a board role, not an operating role — does the system adapt its discovery questions?
- Recruiter-only notes should NEVER appear in candidate-facing output
- Injected primer should shape questions without being detectable by the candidate
