# Lens Project — Core Validation Questions

Use these prompts with any AI research tool (ChatGPT, Gemini, Perplexity, Claude) to pressure-test the Lens Project's foundational assumptions. Each question is self-contained — paste one at a time for deeper results.

---

## 1. Why hasn't deep-fit assessment been built at scale?

> Applicant tracking systems (ATS) dominate hiring technology, but they match on keywords and credentials — not on whether a candidate will actually thrive in a specific role. Psychometric tools like DISC and StrengthsFinder exist but are static, backward-looking, and disconnected from the hiring decision. Structured interviews help but are expensive and happen late in the process.
>
> **My question:** Why hasn't a major HR technology platform built a product that captures a candidate's values, work style, energy sources, and dealbreakers through guided discovery — and then scores job opportunities against that profile in real time? Is this a technical limitation, a market structure problem, a buyer behavior issue, or something else? What have the closest attempts been, and why did they fail or stay small?

---

## 2. Is "fit prediction before hire" the right problem — or is the real bottleneck somewhere else?

> I'm building a product premised on the idea that the core hiring problem isn't sourcing or screening speed — it's that neither the candidate nor the employer has a reliable way to predict whether someone will thrive in a specific seat before they're in it. The cost of getting this wrong is 1.5–2x annual salary for the employer and months of misery for the candidate.
>
> **My question:** When CHROs, Heads of Talent Acquisition, and VPs of People talk about their biggest hiring challenges, is "predicting fit before the offer" actually what keeps them up at night? Or is the real pain somewhere else — time-to-fill, candidate experience, internal mobility, retention after 18 months, manager quality, something I'm not seeing? What does the research say about where the most value is destroyed in the hiring lifecycle?

---

## 3. Does bidirectional matching exist anywhere — and what would it take to make it work?

> Most hiring tools are one-directional: the employer defines a job description, candidates apply, and a system ranks them. Some career tools flip this — the candidate defines preferences and gets matched to openings. But almost nothing runs structured discovery on *both* sides and scores the match bidirectionally.
>
> **My question:** Are there any existing products, academic research programs, or enterprise pilots that attempt true bidirectional matching — where both the candidate and the role/company go through a deep discovery process, and a match score is generated from both profiles? If this has been attempted, what happened? If it hasn't, what are the structural barriers? Consider the perspectives of enterprise HR buyers, recruiting platforms, coaching/assessment firms, and candidates themselves.

---

## 4. What is the market receptivity for AI-guided career coaching that produces a functional artifact?

> Career coaching has an ROI problem — it's hard to measure whether coaching "worked." My product uses AI-guided discovery (optionally facilitated by a human coach's encoded methodology) to produce a concrete output: a portable "lens document" that captures a person's professional identity — values, work style, energy patterns, dealbreakers, mission alignment — in a structured, machine-readable format. That document then governs automated job scoring and matching.
>
> **My question:** What is the current state of the AI coaching market? Who are the players, what are they producing, and how are they measuring outcomes? Is there evidence that coaching produces better outcomes when it generates a functional artifact (like a profile or action plan) versus when it's purely conversational? What do we know about willingness to pay — both from individual job seekers and from enterprise buyers (employers, outplacement firms, coaching practices)?

---

## 5. What does the competitive landscape look like for "identity-first" career tools — and where are the gaps?

> The career technology market is crowded with job boards (Indeed, LinkedIn), ATS platforms (Greenhouse, Lever, Ashby), assessment tools (DISC, Gallup, Hogan), AI resume builders, and a new wave of AI career assistants (like Jack & Jill AI, which raised $20M). But most of these optimize for speed or volume — finding more jobs faster, screening more candidates quicker.
>
> **My question:** Map the competitive landscape for tools that prioritize *depth of self-understanding* over *speed of job matching*. Who is building products that help candidates or employers understand fit at a deeper level than skills and experience? Include: AI coaching platforms, next-gen assessment companies, "career identity" or "career design" startups, and any enterprise tools focused on internal mobility or culture-fit scoring. For each, identify what they produce, how they measure success, their pricing model, and where their ceiling is. I'm specifically looking for the gap between recruiter-level intelligence (fast, surface, keyword-driven) and coaching-level depth (slow, identity-driven, signal-based).
