## The Problem

Every time someone applies for a job, they reverse-engineer their resume to pass keyword filters. By the time it reaches a hiring manager, it reveals presentation skill, not fit. Interviews compensate, but they're expensive, inconsistent, and come too late. Mis-hires cost 1.5 to 2 times annual salary.

The gap isn't in filtering — ATS systems filter at scale. The gap is in what's being filtered. Every tool in the hiring stack operates on the same shallow input: keywords, titles, credentials, assessment scores. Better filtering of shallow data still produces shallow results. None of these tools answer the question that predicts retention: will this person thrive here, in this role, on this team, at this stage?

## What Exists

ATS systems (Rippling, Lever, Ashby) filter thousands of applicants down to hundreds using keyword overlap. They may have internal post-ATS narrowing tools — but those tools still operate on the same resume-derived input. Personality assessments (DISC, MBTI) produce self-reported traits with no directionality: they describe you but don't match you to anything. The nearest AI competitor, Jack & Jill ($20M seed, ~50K users), does wide but not deep: resume upload, light preference matching, broad job discovery. Better UX than job boards, same depth ceiling.

The structural gap: no existing tool collects the data that would make better matching possible. Behavioral signals, energy patterns, builder-vs-maintainer orientation, values in practice, disqualifiers — this information never enters the system. You can't filter on what was never captured.

## What a Lens Is

A lens is a structured document that captures professional identity at a level resumes never reach. It's built from multiple sources — resume, LinkedIn, assessments, writing samples, coaching recordings, and anything else that reveals how someone actually works. An AI-guided or coach-facilitated discovery conversation (25 to 45 minutes) pushes past surface answers, cross-referencing uploaded materials with conversational responses to catch contradictions and blind spots neither source would surface alone.

Six dimensions that predict fit and retention: the throughline across someone's career that titles don't show; values demonstrated through behavior, not stated on a careers page; actual work style and energy patterns — the strongest predictor of retention that nobody measures; builder versus maintainer orientation (hiring the wrong one for your stage is the most common mis-hire in growth companies); and the disqualifiers they'd never put on a resume.

The output is a portable markdown document with structured metadata — machine-readable, human-readable, owned by the person. We call it a lens because it changes what you see. The same opportunity looks completely different through a document that knows what fills you versus what drains you.

## The Feedback Loop

A lens is not a static report. It's a living document designed to improve through use. Connected to a scoring pipeline, the lens evaluates opportunities daily against identity signals. Every action — pursuing a match, skipping one, declining an offer — feeds back into the system. When scoring results feel wrong, the user goes back and enriches their lens. The pipeline creates the incentive that makes the lens worth investing in.

This is the critical design point: the free standalone lens is a demonstration of the methodology. The lens connected to a pipeline is the product. Without the pipeline, a sophisticated user could achieve comparable throughline analysis by prompting ChatGPT directly. With the pipeline, the lens becomes an operational tool that works daily — and that daily use drives continuous enrichment.

## Signal Matching Over Keyword Matching

A resume answers "what have you done?" A lens answers "who are you and where will you thrive?" A resume is disposable — rewritten for every application. A lens is indelible — the durable version of who you are, persisting across roles, industries, and life stages. Your titles change. Your skills evolve. Your essence doesn't.

Quality ceiling: a lens is only as good as what goes into it. Coach-facilitated discovery goes deeper than self-guided. Multi-source input (assessments, writing samples, recommendations) is harder to game than single-channel self-report. But the strongest anti-gaming mechanism is intrinsic motivation: when your lens governs your daily job matches, shallow input costs you real opportunities. The system rewards honest engagement because honest engagement produces better results.

## Bidirectional Scoring

The core intellectual property: both sides build a lens. A candidate creates one through guided discovery. A hiring team creates a role lens — not a job description, but a structured document capturing who actually thrives in this seat, the team's real culture, and the genuine disqualifiers. A scoring engine evaluates fit in both directions simultaneously. The result: not "does this person have the right keywords" but "will this relationship work?"

Asymmetric fit becomes visible — a candidate might be strong for the company while the company is wrong for them. Standard hiring never surfaces this. Provisional patent filed March 2026 (App #64/015,187). Honest caveat: the employer-mandated use case is structurally weaker than self-directed. Multi-source cross-referencing and coaching mitigate gaming, but intrinsic motivation is the real mechanism. The employer side works best for internal mobility (where the employee is motivated) rather than external screening.

## Where It Stands

Intake form: live, being tested with 13+ testers across job seekers, passive searchers, coaches, and HR practitioners. Candidate-to-role scorer: operational, running daily. Role-to-candidate scorer: built with 9 evaluation modes, in testing. Bidirectional matching spec: documented, not yet production. Patent: provisional filed. Not built yet: production multi-user infrastructure, longitudinal outcome data, employer deployment. Working prototype with promising single-user results. The question is whether it generalizes.
