# Lens Project -- Executive Search Validation Guide
## Conversation Framework for Retained Search and Board Placement Professionals
### v1.0 | April 13, 2026

---

## How to Use This Guide

This is a conversation framework, not a script. Read it before the call. Pick 3-5 questions that fit the person and the flow of conversation. The goal is to learn what these professionals see from their desk -- not to pitch the Lens Project. If the concept resonates, they'll ask you to tell them more. If it doesn't, you need to hear that clearly.

**Your posture:** You're building something early-stage. You have a hypothesis. You want their honest reaction. You are not an expert in their field. They are.

**Time budget:** 20-30 minutes. Don't overstay. If it's going well, they'll extend.

---

## Context You Carry In (Don't Lead With This)

These are things you know. Hold them. Let the conversation confirm, challenge, or reframe them.

- Edie Hunt (retired Goldman Sachs partner, 30+ years): deep discovery is only justified at $300K+/board-level placements. Exec recruiters are the likely buyer, not HR and not coaches.
- James Pratt (career coach): companies prioritize hiring volume over precision. The pain of a bad hire is diffuse; the pain of an open seat is immediate.
- SquarePeg (2016) tried psychometric bidirectional matching with Fortune 100 clients. Pivoted to AI resume screening. The fit-matching thesis didn't hold at their price point.
- The competitive landscape has no product in the "deep + scalable" quadrant. Internal mobility tools (Gloat, Fuel50, Eightfold) match on skills only. Coaching tools don't produce machine-readable artifacts.

## Opening (2-3 Minutes)

Thank them for the time. Acknowledge the intro. Keep the setup under 60 seconds.

"I spent 13 years as the first customer-facing hire at a B2B SaaS company, and through that experience and a year-long job search, I became acutely aware of how much signal gets lost between who someone actually is and what a resume communicates. I'm building a tool that uses AI-guided conversation to capture that deeper signal -- values, work style, energy, dealbreakers -- in a structured format. I'm early stage, and I'm trying to understand whether the people who evaluate senior professionals for a living see the same gap I do."

Then stop talking. Let them react.

## Core Questions

### 1. The Gap (Their Experience)

*What you're testing: Does the gap between resume and reality feel real to someone who evaluates candidates professionally?*

"When you're evaluating a senior candidate -- VP, C-level -- what do you learn in the first 90 minutes of conversation that you couldn't have learned from their resume and LinkedIn?"

**Follow-up if they engage:**
- "How do you capture that signal? Is it in your notes, your head, a structured format?"
- "If a candidate gave you that signal upfront -- structured, written -- would that save you time or would it feel like it was bypassing your process?"

**What you're listening for:**
- Whether they describe the gap in terms similar to yours (values, style, energy) or in completely different terms (network, references, track record)
- Whether they see the gap as a problem they want solved or a feature of their value-add (if it's the latter, you're threatening their moat, not solving their problem)

---

### 2. The Failure Modes (What Goes Wrong)

*What you're testing: Where does the hiring process actually break for the placements they handle?*

"In your experience, when a placement doesn't work out -- the person leaves within 12-18 months -- what was typically the miss? Was it something that could have been caught earlier?"

**Follow-up if they engage:**
- "Is it usually a candidate issue, a company/team issue, or a mismatch that both sides missed?"
- "Do you see patterns in what gets missed -- culture, manager fit, scope expectations, something else?"

**What you're listening for:**
- Whether the failure modes map to identity signals (values, style, energy) or operational signals (scope, comp, title)
- Whether they believe earlier/deeper candidate profiling would have caught the miss, or whether the problem is fundamentally on the company side

---

### 3. The Candidate Side (What They Wish They Had)

*What you're testing: Would a lens-like document be useful to a search professional, and at what stage?*

"If you could have a structured document for every candidate -- not their resume, but a profile of how they work, what energizes them, what they need from a team, what would make them leave -- would you use that? At what point in your process?"

**Follow-up if they engage:**
- "Would you trust it if the candidate authored it themselves through a guided process, or would you need to have done the assessment yourself?"
- "Would it change how you present candidates to clients?"

**What you're listening for:**
- Whether they'd use it as intake signal (early), validation signal (mid), or not at all
- Whether the trust issue is about the format or about who authored it (this is critical -- if they don't trust candidate-authored identity documents, the product needs to go through the recruiter, not around them)

---

### 4. The Company Side (The Harder Problem)

*What you're testing: Is the employer self-assessment problem as bad as the research suggests?*

"One of the hardest things I've found is getting companies to articulate what it's actually like to sit in a specific seat on a specific team -- beyond the job description. Do you run into that? How do you extract that information?"

**Follow-up if they engage:**
- "If you had a structured profile of the team and role -- management style, operating cadence, real autonomy level -- would that change how you match?"
- "Who on the company side is most honest about this -- the hiring manager, HR, the outgoing person?"

**What you're listening for:**
- Whether they've developed their own methods for extracting honest team/role profiles (if yes, you're building something they already do manually)
- Whether they see the company-side problem as solvable or structural

---

### 5. The Market Question (Who Would Pay)

*What you're testing: Is there a buying motion here, and who holds the budget?*

"If a tool existed that produced this kind of depth -- structured identity profiles for candidates, or for roles -- who would pay for it in your world? The search firm? The client company? The candidate?"

**Follow-up if they engage:**
- "What would it need to demonstrate before you'd consider using it in your practice?"
- "Is there a price point where it becomes interesting versus a distraction?"

**What you're listening for:**
- Whether the buyer is the firm (tool for their practice), the client (better hire), or the candidate (better self-presentation) -- this determines the entire GTM
- Whether the value prop is time savings, placement quality, or reduced failure rate

---

### 6. The Competitive Lens (What They've Seen)

*What you're testing: Are there tools or approaches in the search world that overlap with what you're building?*

"Have you seen any tools, platforms, or approaches in the last few years that try to go deeper than the resume -- whether that's AI-based, assessment-based, or something else? What worked? What didn't?"

**What you're listening for:**
- Tools you don't know about (search firms often have proprietary tools or niche vendor relationships)
- Whether the industry has tried and rejected something similar, or whether this is genuinely unoccupied space

---

## If the Conversation is Going Well (Optional, Read the Room)

### 7. The Demo Hook

"I have a working prototype -- would you be open to trying the discovery process yourself, or having someone on your team try it? It takes about 45-60 minutes. I'd love your reaction to the output."

**Only ask this if:**
- They've shown genuine curiosity (asking you questions back)
- They've validated at least one core assumption
- The conversation has gone past 20 minutes naturally

### 8. The Network Ask

"Is there anyone else in the executive search world you think I should talk to about this -- especially someone who might see it differently than you do?"

**Always ask this.** Even if the conversation was lukewarm. Especially if it was lukewarm.
